# Negotiation Prep

The seller's preparation layer before and during the commercial exchange. Covers the
concessions ladder (planned give-gets, not ad hoc reactions), BATNA awareness,
procurement navigation, and the closing checklist.

> **Governing rules:**
> - `rules/common/selling-principles.md` — evidence-first; no invented claims or
>   fabricated concession math.
> - `rules/approval-matrix.md` — which discount or non-standard term requires whose
>   sign-off BEFORE it is offered. This skill does not price deals; it cites
>   `quote-desk` for all concession economics and approval requirements.
> - `rules/meddpicc/deal-review.md` — red/amber/green scoring of deal health.
>   Read the current deal-review score FIRST; do not prepare to close a deal with
>   a red economic buyer or a red paper process.

Negotiation prep sits between the agreed commercial proposal and signature. It is
the moment to confirm MEDDPICC is solid, sequence the give-get ladder deliberately,
and know the walk-away before the buyer names a number.

## When to Activate

Activate this skill when:

- A deal is moving from Validation/Proof to Proposal/Negotiation
  (`rules/lifecycle-stages.md` stage 4).
- A buyer or procurement team has pushed back on price, terms, or scope.
- A rep asks "what can I give on" or "what is my concession strategy".
- Discount or non-standard term requests arrive and the rep needs to know
  the approval tier before responding (`rules/approval-matrix.md`).
- BATNA needs to be mapped (what does the buyer do if this deal fails?).
- Procurement or legal is now the active counter-party.

Do **not** activate for:
- Pricing and discount math — that is `quote-desk`.
- Building the mutual milestone timeline — that is `close-plan`.
- Tracking the status of legal documents (MSA, DPA) — that is `paper-process`.
- Initial MEDDPICC qualification — that is the discovery/qualification stage.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Confirm MEDDPICC is solid before preparing concessions

1. Pull the current deal-review score from `rules/meddpicc/deal-review.md`.
   Check each element:
   - **E (Economic buyer):** Named, met, engaged, and on the close plan? If red,
     stop — preparing a concession ladder for a deal with no confirmed EB is
     preparing to give value away to a coach, not a decision-maker.
   - **D (Decision process):** Do you know every approval step and its owner?
     If amber or red, the negotiation timetable is unknowable.
   - **P (Paper process):** Has `paper-process` been opened? If not, start it
     now — paper can extend the timeline by weeks and undermine a close date.
   - **C (Champion):** Is the champion selling internally while you are in
     procurement? If red, negotiate slowly; you may be negotiating a deal
     nobody is buying internally.
2. Flag any red/amber as a risk in the prep output. Do not paper over gaps with
   concessions — a discount does not fix a missing EB.

### Step 2 — Build the concessions ladder

A concessions ladder is a pre-committed sequence of give-gets (these are concession rungs — distinct from quote-desk's approval Tiers), not a list of
things you are willing to give away. Every give has a corresponding get.

1. **Identify the buyer's known pressure points** from discovery and deal notes.
   Common levers: price, payment terms, implementation timeline, contract length,
   seat count, SLA tier, professional services scope.
2. **Rank your levers by cost-to-give vs. value-to-receive.** Low cost to give,
   high value to buyer = offer early as a show of flexibility. High cost to give =
   hold until the buyer has committed something material in return.
3. **Map each lever to the approval tier** from `rules/approval-matrix.md`:
   - Rep self-serve (log it), Sales Manager, VP Sales, CRO + Finance.
   - Do not offer a concession that requires VP Sales approval without first
     routing the request. The buyer hearing "I need to check" after you offered
     it is worse than not offering it.
4. **Set the walk-away position.** The walk-away is the BATNA floor, not a bluff.
   Document what you will not give and why. A deal won below that floor is a deal
   that hurts the business.
5. **Route any required approvals to `quote-desk` before the negotiation session.**
   Concession economics (discount percentages, net ARR impact, payment term NPV)
   are calculated there, not here.

Common give-get pairs:

| Give | Get |
|---|---|
| Accelerated discount | Shorter payment terms (net-30 vs. net-60) |
| Multi-year lock-in price | Signed order form by [date] |
| Extended payment schedule | Increased ACV or expanded seats |
| Professional services credit | Early signature commitment |
| Pilot / proof of concept | Named case study right (with approval) |

> **Guardrail:** The left column items all require `rules/approval-matrix.md`
> approval at their tier before they are offered. Never offer first, approve second.

### Step 3 — Map procurement and the counter-party

1. Identify who is driving the commercial negotiation:
   - Procurement / CPO / vendor management team (price + terms focus).
   - Legal (redlines and contract language).
   - Finance (payment, revenue recognition, budget release).
2. Determine their objectives. Procurement wants defensible price reduction and
   contract compliance. Legal wants liability limits and data terms. Finance wants
   budget certainty. Each counter-party has a different definition of "win".
3. Check `stakeholder-mapping` for the buying-committee structure — procurement
   is rarely the economic buyer but can block or delay a deal indefinitely.
   Know who procurement reports to and who has the authority to override a stall.
4. Confirm whether the champion is engaged with procurement. A champion who cannot
   influence the procurement team is a coach; treat the risk accordingly
   (`rules/meddpicc/deal-review.md`).

### Step 4 — Prepare the closing checklist

Before entering any negotiation session, confirm:

- [ ] Economic buyer is confirmed and engaged in the close.
- [ ] Decision process and paper process are mapped (`paper-process` is open).
- [ ] Close date on the mutual action plan is agreed with the buyer.
- [ ] All required approvals for the concessions you may offer are pre-obtained.
- [ ] Walk-away position is documented and understood by the rep's manager.
- [ ] Champion has been briefed and is actively selling internally.
- [ ] Competition / BATNA is known — do you know what the buyer does if this deal
   fails? What is their status quo cost?

### Step 5 — BATNA awareness

1. **Your BATNA:** What does losing this deal mean for your number? Is this a
   commit-category deal (`rules/meddpicc/forecast-risk.md`)? Knowing your own
   BATNA tells you how much flexibility you actually have.
2. **Buyer's BATNA:** What does the buyer do if this deal does not close — stay
   on the incumbent, build in-house, go to a competitor? A buyer with a weak
   BATNA (incumbent is painful, status quo is costly, board mandate is active)
   has less leverage than they project.
3. **Competition mapping:** Reference `competitor-battlecards` for the live
   competitive position. Do not invent claims about competitors; use only
   approved differentiation.
4. **Urgency:** Is there a genuine, buyer-acknowledged reason this closes now?
   Manufactured urgency ("our quarter ends Friday") is transparent and corrosive;
   real urgency (their implementation window, a compliance deadline, a board
   approval cycle) is legitimate leverage — use it with evidence.

## Examples

**Concession ladder for an enterprise software deal:**

```text
Scenario: $120k ARR deal, buyer asking for 25% discount.
Rep has a Sales Manager approval limit of 20% (< $50k ACV threshold is not
applicable here at $120k — routes to VP Sales per rules/approval-matrix.md).

Step 1 — MEDDPICC check:
  E: CFO confirmed as EB, met in week 6. GREEN.
  D: Two-step: VP sign, then CFO sign. Dates unclear — AMBER. Probe before session.
  P: paper-process opened; MSA first draft outstanding. AMBER.
  Champion: VP of Ops is strong advocate. GREEN.

Step 2 — concession ladder (pre-approved by Sales Manager; VP approval obtained
  for up to 22% via quote-desk routing):
  Rung 1 (offer if they push at all): payment terms improvement — net-45 vs. net-60.
    Cost: low. Value to Finance: high.
  Rung 2 (offer if Rung 1 declined): 10% discount in exchange for
    a 2-year commit and signed order form by [date].
  Rung 3 (hold for genuine impasse): 15% discount in exchange for
    reference / case study right (marketing must pre-approve) + 2-year.
  Walk-away: 22% is the ceiling (VP-approved); any further requires CRO + Finance.
    Below 22% on a 1-year deal is not offered.

Step 3 — procurement counter-party:
  Procurement lead: [Name], reports to CFO.
  Their objective: 20% reduction against a comparable vendor quote they hold.
  Champion action: VP Ops has briefed CFO on strategic fit; CFO is supportive
    but expects procurement to "do their job."

Step 4 — closing checklist:
  [x] EB confirmed
  [x] VP approval obtained for up to 22%
  [ ] Close date agreed — still amber; confirm with champion before session
  [x] Walk-away set at 22% / 2-year
  [ ] paper-process: MSA draft outstanding — flag for legal before session
```

**Procurement pushback — "your competitor is 30% cheaper":**

```text
Procurement: Competitor X is 30% cheaper. Match it or we go with them.
Rep prep:
  1. Do not react with a counter-offer on the spot.
  2. Verify the competitor claim via competitor-battlecards (approved diff only).
  3. Identify what "30% cheaper" means — same scope? Same SLA? Same contract length?
     Procurement often compares headline price, not TCO.
  4. Re-anchor on buyer's stated success criteria from discovery (their Metrics, their
     Decision Criteria from MEDDPICC) — map where the product's differentiation lands
     against those criteria.
  5. Offer Rung 1 give-get (e.g. payment terms) without touching price first.
     If they hold firm, route to Rung 2 via quote-desk / approval obtained.
  6. Never match a fabricated competitive price. If the competitor genuinely is
     cheaper for the same scope, name the trade-offs honestly; do not invent
     claims about the competitor (selling-principles §2).
```

**Walk-away triggered:**

```text
Buyer: We need 40% or the deal is off.
Rep: 40% requires CRO + Finance approval (rules/approval-matrix.md). I can take it
  there, but I want to be honest — that level of discount requires a very specific
  business case, and I am not confident it will be approved for a 1-year term.
  Can we talk about what a 2-year or 3-year structure looks like? That gives Finance
  a different lens on the ask.
  [If buyer refuses to consider multi-year and holds at 40% — escalate to manager;
   do NOT offer 40% without CRO + Finance approval recorded.]
```

## Anti-patterns

- **Offering concessions without prior approval.** Saying "I can do 25%" before
  `quote-desk` has run the math and the approval has been obtained violates
  `rules/approval-matrix.md` and creates a commitment you may not be able to honor.
- **Preparing to close without checking MEDDPICC.** A concession ladder on a deal
  with a red economic buyer is not negotiation preparation; it is discount theater.
  Check `rules/meddpicc/deal-review.md` before you build the ladder.
- **Treating procurement as the enemy.** Procurement is a stakeholder with a job.
  Understand their objective (defensible price + contract compliance) and give them
  a path to a win — leverage `stakeholder-mapping` to understand who they report to.
- **Manufactured urgency.** "Our quarter ends Friday" is transparent and erodes trust.
  Use only genuine, buyer-acknowledged urgency with evidence.
- **One-sided concessions.** Every give needs a get. A concession with no reciprocal
  ask trains the buyer that asking yields unilateral movement.
- **Inventing competitor claims.** "Competitor X can't do Y" is only statable with
  approved, sourced differentiation from `competitor-battlecards`. Never fabricate.
- **Skipping the walk-away step.** Knowing the floor after the buyer names their
  number is too late. Document the walk-away before the session.
